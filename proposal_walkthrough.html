Location Signal — What the Proposal Changes

A visual walkthrough of what Wiom's system will do after the build. Focused on what the system does, not how it's engineered. Companion to solution_frame_v6.md and the two Gate 0 contracts.

§0 — Why this proposal exists

Two customer complaints land on Wiom's doorstep every week:

"Mujhe WiFi chahiye but Wiom mana kar raha hai." (I want WiFi but Wiom is refusing me.)
"Mera connection aaj lagna tha, but koi lagane nahi aaya." (My connection was supposed to install today, but nobody came.)

Behind these two complaints sit two decisions Wiom gets structurally wrong today:

  • Point A — Wiom's promise decision. Wiom commits on one un-interrogated GPS tap. 25.7% of installed bookings drift beyond GPS apparatus noise — the coord Wiom commits on often isn't the customer's home.
  • Point B — the partner's install decision. The partner receives an unstructured text address and has to rebuild the landmark → gali → floor chain on a voice call. 40.7% of classifiable partner-customer pairs have a first call just about location. 77.5% of those calls still end in confusion.

Serviceability shapes the ceiling. 55.3% of bookings inside a partner's serviceable polygon install; 38.6% outside. The partner's familiarity with an area governs whether install succeeds.

What the build targets. Booking → install rate of 40% → ≥49%, jointly contributed by the two workstreams in this proposal (clean capture + structured address). Model activation (BM1/BM2) and partner expansion stack beyond that — tracked separately. Full derivation in l5_target_derivation.md.

This walkthrough describes what the system will do after the build. Companion reads: solution_frame_v6.md (mental model) · problem_statements/problem_1_location_estimation_v3.md and problem_2_address_translation_v3.md (Gate 0 contracts) · master_story.md (evidence backbone).

§2 — How inputs get captured

What the customer does

Instead of one GPS tap + "yes I'm at home", the app asks for structure the customer already has in her head:

  • Pick at least 2 landmarks near home from a nearby list — mandir, kirana, metro, chowk, school — whatever is within 2-3 minutes' walk.
  • Type gali name and floor as structured fields — not a single free-text blob.
  • Optional photo or short video from the landmark to the door, for tighter confirmation when needed.

If no landmark fits ("I don't recognise any of these"), the app tries a different set — Wiom's own install-history landmarks — before falling back to a support call.

What the partner sees

Today the partner sees a map pin + a blob of typed address. After the build, he sees:

  • Customer-confirmed landmark — the actual one she picked.
  • Gali and floor as separate, structured fields.
  • Each landmark flagged against his own history — installed-near-before (inclusion), declined-near-before (exclusion), or new to him.
  • Reference to his own recent installs: "Between Install I882 (Shiv Mandir side, 5 days ago) and I891 (E-Block main road, 2 days ago)."
  • Consequence of declining, shown upfront: the landmarks around this booking move to his exclusion list — his polygon shrinks in this area.
Landmarks are anchors, not labels. The same landmarks that anchor the customer's home truth also travel to the partner as navigable reference points. Each partner accumulates an inclusion list (landmarks he's installed near) and an exclusion list (landmarks he's declined near). When shown a new booking, he instantly sees which landmarks are familiar, which he's declined before, and which are new territory. Captured once, consumed twice.

§3 — How the system determines confidence

Gate 1 — User Address Confidence

Answers: is the customer telling the truth about where her home is? Computed from what she does in the app: number of landmarks confirmed, whether she picked any gaming-defence probes, GPS stream stability during booking, and night-time GPS cluster consistency over subsequent days.

TierWhat it meansWhat happens
HIGH≥2 landmarks confirmed, signals consistent, not at home unlikelyPass to Gate 2, no friction
MIDPartial signal, or GPS noisyRemediate via Street View, photo, or support call. Promote to HIGH → pass. If unrecovered → reject.
LOWNo landmark relates, probe failAsk the customer to voice-affirm she is at home — she says something like "haan mai yahi hu" ("yes, I am here"). If she affirms — pass to Gate 2 on the strength of the voice confirmation (recording stored as evidence; voice carries richer signal than text — tone, hesitation, accent — and is harder to automate fraudulently). If she doesn't — reject. No payment captured either way until Gate 2 resolves.

Gate 2 — Serviceability Confidence

Answers: can a partner actually serve this location? Computed from the partner's own serviceable polygon (drawn from his install + decline history), how dense his installs are around this booking, whether his team's recent movement touches the area, and whether the confirmed landmarks feature in his past installs.

TierWhat it meansWhat happens
HIGHInside a partner's dense polygon, landmarks match his install historyPartner is expected to install. Decline requires him to confirm against a visible consequence — polygon shrinks here.
MIDEdge of polygon — landmarks recognised but partner not yet denseAsk the partner: accept, decline, or verify first with a paid visit. Each option's consequence to his polygon is shown before he confirms.
LOWOutside anyone's serviceable polygonReject — no payment. Booking is recorded to an internal expansion-demand stock (a data signal that informs future partner expansion); customer notified if coverage reaches her later.
Sequential, not parallel. Gate 2 only runs once Gate 1 confirms the coord is a real home. There's no point asking "can a partner serve this?" if we aren't sure it's actually her home.
Gate 2 logic and partner routing evolve over time.
  • Gate 2 tier decision — Phase 1 ships polygon-only containment (is the booking inside any partner's polygon?). A belief model (BM1) then enriches the tier decision with partner-history signals the polygon alone doesn't capture — pricing install density, recency, and landmark-partner match into a richer HIGH / MID / LOW verdict.
  • Route-to-partner — initially rule-based among eligible partners. An allocation ranking model (BM2) then learns from every accept / decline / install / cancel edge to rank partners more accurately over time.
The user-facing flow stays the same — HIGH / MID / LOW with the same action tree. Only the sophistication of how the tier is computed and which partner gets notified first evolves.

§4 — How the system values partner signals

Every partner decision informs the polygon. The outcome maps directly onto whether the polygon expands or shrinks in that hex, and onto whether the booking's landmarks move to the partner's inclusion or exclusion list:

  • Post install (HIGH booking accepted, technician installs) → polygon expands in this hex. Booking's landmarks added to partner's inclusion list. No verification needed — the install is the evidence.
  • Post verify-visit at the margin (MID booking, partner picked "Verify first"):
    • Reached door + install binds → polygon expands. Landmarks move to inclusion list.
    • Couldn't reach / install failed → polygon shrinks. Landmarks move to exclusion list.
  • Post decline without verification (HIGH or MID decline) → polygon shrinks, softer than a verify-visit failure (the partner declined without testing). Consequence shown before he confirms the decline.

Each stock has a declared decay: old decisions weigh less, a single bad day doesn't stain the mobile's jitter profile forever, a polygon that shrank in a hex can grow back on clean evidence. Nothing silently accumulates. And the source of each signal — customer or partner — sees the consequence visible back to them:

  • Customer whose landmark validated sees acknowledgment after install: "The partner reached your home using the Shiv Mandir landmark you confirmed."
  • Partner sees red zones in his app where his declines have reshaped his polygon — before he takes his next decision, not after.
  • Customer sees honest messaging at every outcome: HIGH / MID-with-SLA / LOW rejection, expansion-queue status.

§6 — Partner zones (what serviceable polygons look like today)

Each partner has a serviceable polygon drawn from their own install and decline history. Inside the polygon, install rates are 55.3%; outside, they collapse to 38.6% (master story Part C.D). The build makes these polygons live inputs to Gate 2 and visible to the partner — with shrink/grow consequences shown before every accept/decline/verify decision.

The map below shows actual partner serviceable polygons generated from Wiom's install history, auto-focused on Delhi. Each coloured region is one partner's area of service. What changes after the build. Today these polygons are R&D artefacts — the production gate doesn't consult them. After Phase 1, they become the eligibility gate at Gate 2. After Phase 2, partners see their own polygon and its growth/shrink history live in their app. After Phase 3, every accept / decline / verify-visit redraws the polygon with decay, so the shape stays fresh.
