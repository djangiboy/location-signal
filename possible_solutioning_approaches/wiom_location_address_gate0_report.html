Gate 0 Submission · Wiom Field Ops

Getting the customer's house right

Two related problems with one root cause: we don't know exactly where the customer lives, and the installer in the field doesn't either. This report explains — in plain language — what's going wrong, what other companies did about it, and what we should build.

Repo: genie_stocks Branch: temproal-features Date: 2026-04-16 Stage: Gate 0 — pre-build contract
The one-paragraph summary

A user fills a form on a cheap Android phone. The phone's GPS puts them 500 metres away from where they actually are. Our system reads that wrong location from the database and makes decisions based on it. Later, an installer is handed a raw address like "Flat 3B, Green Apts, near SBI" and has to figure it out alone. Both problems have been solved by other Indian companies — Swiggy for the location problem, DTDC and Ecom Express for the address problem. We don't need to invent anything. We need to copy their playbook and plug it into tools we've already built.

00A day in the life — where things break

Before talking about solutions, let's walk through what actually happens to one customer and one installer. Every failure mode in both projects shows up in this one story.

Morning · 9:12 AM
Priya fills the form

Priya lives in a 3rd-floor flat in Lajpat Nagar. She opens our landing page on her ₹6,000 Android phone. It asks for her location. She taps "allow". The phone fires up GPS — but she's indoors, her building is tall, and the nearest clear sky is blocked. The phone returns a coordinate that is 540 metres to the east, on top of a petrol pump. She doesn't notice. She types "Flat 3B, Green Apts, near SBI, Lajpat Nagar-III" and hits submit.

Morning · 9:13 AM
Our system reads it

Our ingestion pipeline reads two things from Priya's submission: her lat/lon (the petrol pump, wrong) and her address text (correct, but messy). It stores both in Snowflake. No one checks whether the two agree. The lead scoring model uses the wrong lat/lon to look up hex-level install history — which belongs to the petrol pump, not Priya's building. The score is noisy. Problem 1 starts here.

Afternoon · 2:30 PM
The installer gets the task

Ramesh, a CSP field agent, opens his app and sees a new install task. It shows one field: the raw address string Priya typed. No map pin. No building reference. No neighbourhood context. He has to mentally parse "Flat 3B, Green Apts, near SBI, Lajpat Nagar-III" and drive there. There are three SBI branches in Lajpat Nagar and two buildings called "Green Apartments". He picks one and heads out. Problem 2 starts here.

Evening · 4:45 PM
The install fails

Ramesh is at the wrong SBI, at the wrong Green Apts. He calls Priya. She can't describe landmarks over the phone. After 20 minutes he finds the right building — but by then he's late for his next task and has to reschedule Priya's install for tomorrow. Cost to us: one wasted trip, one angry customer, one CSP losing an hour of productive time, one extra support call. Multiply by thousands of leads.

What this story reveals The customer's phone can't be fixed — it's cheap hardware, bad sky, concrete walls. But three things are true in parallel: (a) we had enough other signals to catch the bad GPS before using it, (b) we had enough other signals to give the installer a better address than raw text, and (c) we never composed any of those signals. 01What we already have in the repo

The good news: we don't need to build much from scratch. We already have six pieces lying around in genie_stocks that were built for other purposes (scoring, reporting) but turn out to be exactly what we need for a trust layer and an address packet. Think of them as tools already in the toolbox — we just have to pick them up.

Tool 1
Partner service zones

For each CSP we already know the polygon of where they actually work — built from clustering their past installs.
File: partner_cluster_boundaries.h5

Tool 2
Install-density map

Every city is tiled into small hexagons (50–500m). Each hex knows how many installs happened there. Tells us where real customers live.

Tool 3
Ward boundaries

We already spatial-join every lead to a municipal ward polygon. Good for disambiguating "which Sarojini Nagar?" cases.

Every customer we've ever installed is a verified address. This is our gold-standard reference, for free. Stored in Snowflake, queryable by pincode.

02Project 1 — Location Trust Layer

The simple idea: before we use a lat/lon, we ask three quick questions about it. If all three pass, the point is trustworthy. If one or more fail, we either fix the point using the address text, or we ask the customer to confirm on a map. We don't try to make the phone's GPS more accurate — we just refuse to trust bad points blindly.

One-line analogy It's like a bank flagging a suspicious transaction. The ATM still works — we just don't auto-approve anything that looks fishy. Same with coordinates: accept the trustworthy ones, double-check the rest.

Current state (as-is)

Figure 1.1 — Today: lat/lon flows straight from Snowflake into scoring and dispatch with no validation step. A point 2 km off the actual address produces the same decision as a point 5 m off.

Why "fix the device's GPS" is the wrong frame

Constraint: we cannot change the customer's phone, chipset, or the sky above them. Cheap Android in dense urban India produces multipath-contaminated fixes of 60–150 m typical, 2–3 km worst case. Throwing Kalman filters or sensor fusion at ingestion is impossible because we only see the already-landed lat/lon, not the raw satellite measurements. The fix has to live after the bad fix arrives — by detecting that it is bad.
Figure 1.0 — The same customer, three environments. The red scatter on the right is not one broken phone — it's what every cheap Android produces when it sees reflected signals instead of line-of-sight to satellites. This is the physical reality we have to detect and route around. Ranges illustrative; see Hindawi (2014), FocalPoint (2024), Swiggy Bytes (2022) for measured ranges.

What other Indian companies did about this

We are not the first people to run into this. Three well-known Indian companies have written about their fix — with real numbers we can benchmark against.

Swiggy · 2022
"Is this lat/lon believable?"

Swiggy trained a small self-supervised classifier that reads the GPS point, address text, and numeric signals from past delivery interactions, and predicts whether the two are dissonant. Their published model hits AUC-ROC 0.89 with +23 pp precision and +32 pp recall over the address-text-only baseline. No manual labels — signals come from delivery-partner behaviour. This is exactly what we want to copy.

Swiggy · 2023
"If it's not believable, here's a better guess"

Pre-filtered dissonant leads go to a geocoder that proposes a corrected location using past successful deliveries in the same address cluster — benchmarked against a simple DBSCAN of historical delivery points. Crucially, they only correct addresses flagged as inaccurate, never rewrite a customer-supplied point that's fine. That's our "geocode from text" path for MED-trust leads.

Dunzo · 2021
Building-aware dispatch + pin-drop

Dunzo rebuilt their location stack on Google Maps Platform and cut total location-related support calls by 90%. Within that, a concrete 9.3% came specifically from showing delivery partners building names rather than street/pin, and 14% fewer missed ETAs. For broken cases, they ask the customer to tap a pin on a map. That's our LOW-trust fallback.

03Project 2 — CSP Multi-Anchor Address Packet

Today the installer gets one thing: the raw address string the customer typed. The simple idea: give the installer five or six small, independent clues instead of one long, confusing sentence. If one clue is bad, the others still work.

One-line analogy It's like giving directions to a friend. You don't just say "Flat 3B, Green Apts, near SBI". You also say "it's the blue building, 50 metres past the school, same lane where Rohit installed Wiom last month". Each extra clue reduces the chance they get lost. That's all the packet is — multiple clues, delivered together.

Current state (as-is)

Figure 2.1 — Today: the CSP gets one free-text field and has to guess. Every failure mode downstream (calls, re-visits, wrong-address closes) traces back to missing structure at handoff.

Why plain text fails so often in India

Indian addresses are written by humans, for humans, in a hurry. Five specific things go wrong all the time:

What goes wrongExampleWhy the installer can't fix it alone
Street names repeat across localities "Main Road" exists in dozens of places No map app can pick the right one without a pincode or ward
Customer typed the wrong pincode Typed 110024, actually lives in 110025 ~30–40% of Indian pincodes in forms are wrong
No schema for unit number "Flat 3B", "3B Flat", "F3B", "Door 3B" Installer has to guess which part is the unit
Vague landmarks "near SBI", "opposite petrol pump" There are usually multiple — which SBI? which petrol pump?
Abbreviations and short forms "Lj Ngr", "L-III", "Opp. Metro Stn" Map apps don't recognise them

What other Indian companies did about this

The "messy address in India" problem is old. Four concrete fixes already exist in the market; we can stand on top of them, and we have receipts on how well they work.

Pidge + w3w · 2022
3-word codes (what3words)

Every 3×3 metre square on earth has a unique 3-word code (e.g. "filled.count.soap"). You can say it on the phone, type it, or share it on WhatsApp. After Pidge started accepting w3w addresses alongside raw text, first-time delivery failures from address inaccuracy dropped 63% within their first quarter. Available in 12 South Asian languages including Hindi, Marathi, Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, Kannada.

Shiprocket · 2025 · free
Indian address parser (NER)

Shiprocket open-sourced two Indian-address NER models on Hugging Face under Apache 2.0: IndicBERT (accurate, cultural context) and TinyBERT (lightweight, runs on CPU). They tag 11 entity types — building_name, unit, floor, landmark, sub_locality, locality, road, city, state, pincode, country. We literally download the weights and call .predict(text).

DTDC, Ecom, Blue Dart · 2022–23
Two representations, not one

DTDC (MyDTDC app, 2M+ downloads), Ecom Express, and Blue Dart (55,400 pincodes in India) all now accept a w3w address alongside the raw address. The driver's app displays both. They didn't replace the raw address — they augmented it. That's the packet model.

India Post pilot · 2018
3-words in the hands of postmen

India Post ran a 3-week pilot across Maharashtra, Kerala, and Odisha — 2,500 km of routes, 6 post offices, postmen with 20+ years' experience. Findings: 87% of untrained users found it easy to discover their 3-word address, 86% memorability, and 91% said 3-word addresses were easier to communicate than alphanumeric codes. Strong signal that the format works for Indian CSPs too.

Google Maps India · 2023
Landmark-first addressing

Google launched "Address Descriptors" in India — their reverse-geocoding API now returns a pre-selected landmark and area name for any lat/lon, plus 4 alternative landmarks ranked by relevance. Their own data: 98.5% success rate on address queries in India. The reason it works: Indians genuinely navigate by landmarks ("behind the post office"), not by street addresses. Strengthens our landmark field in the packet.

Google Gemini · 2024
GenAI address cleaning

Google published a reference architecture using Gemini on Vertex AI to clean Indian addresses: remove filler ("H.No", "C/O"), expand abbreviations ("rd" → "road"), dedupe city/state, extract landmarks into a separate field, and flag missing pincodes. A usable fallback for addresses that break our NER model.

05Things that could go wrong, and things we don't know yet

Being honest before we build. These are the six biggest concerns, split into "risks we'll have to mitigate" and "questions we need to answer before Gate 1".

Risk 1
New partners have no install history

Checks A (cluster) and C (nearest install) both need past data. For a brand-new partner in a new city, we'll have nothing. Plan: fall back to ward + pincode-centroid checks until history accumulates. Worst case for new partners = what we do today. No regression.

Risk 2
Pincode boundaries drift

India Post changes pincode coverage irregularly. Our centroid table can go stale. Plan: refresh quarterly. Watch for pincodes where install density has two clumps — that's usually a sign the boundary moved.

Risk 3
The CSP app ignores the new fields

If we ship a beautiful packet but the installer's app still shows one blob of text, nothing changes. This project absolutely depends on CSP app team rendering the new fields prominently. Treat it as a hard dependency, not a follow-up.

Risk 4
what3words costs per lookup

It's a paid API. For millions of leads that adds up. Plan: evaluate Google's free Plus Codes as alternative; or only call w3w for MED/LOW trust leads where it actually adds value.

Question 1
Does downstream actually use lat/lon?

Before we claim Project 1 will lift install rate, we need to trace where lat/lon is actually consumed — scoring features? dispatch? serviceability? If the answer is "nowhere that matters", fixing lat/lon is a no-op. This audit is the first thing to do in week 1.

Question 2
What's today's clarification-call rate?

We're targeting a 30–40% drop in clarification calls. But we don't actually know the current number. Pull 30 days of CRM call logs tagged "address clarification" before writing the Gate 0 target.

Multimodal Location-Inaccuracy Classifier

The model that decides whether a customer's GPS pin agrees with the address they typed — without a human labelling anything.

Swiggy's classifier is the closest production-scale analogue of our trust score. Understanding its three design choices — multimodal inputs, self-supervised labels, and pre-filter placement — is worth more than any paper summary.

Figure 7.1 — Swiggy's multimodal architecture. Three input streams (geohashed location, BERT-encoded text, hand-engineered numeric DP signals) get fused into a joint embedding, which a small classifier head scores. Threshold becomes the pre-filter for the geocoder.
Why three modalities beat one

Swiggy's paper reports a baseline that used only location + text (a Siamese-style dissonance detector). Adding the numeric DP signals — things like how often this address has generated "couldn't find customer" calls in the past, how far the DP stopped from the pin on past deliveries — raised precision by 23 pp and recall by 32 pp. The lesson is that operational telemetry (what humans actually do when the data is wrong) is a stronger signal than the data itself.

Numeric signals Swiggy extracts (partial list)
Historical distance between DP's delivery stop and the customer's pin · fraction of past orders at this address where the DP called the customer · time-on-site variance at this address · ratio of orders cancelled "couldn't locate" at this pincode · days since last successful delivery to this address.
Every one of these is free — it falls out of normal operations.
Training dry-run — how Swiggy assembles a single example

The interesting bit is that the label isn't "is the address wrong?" — it's "did the DP behave like the address was wrong?". Below is what a training row looks like conceptually.

7.2 · Swiggy Bytes · 2022

Self-supervised label generation — the geohash-swap trick

How to train a classifier on millions of examples without a single human annotator. This is the single cleverest technique in the Swiggy stack.

The problem: you need labelled examples of "location and address disagree" (positive) and "they agree" (negative). Hand-labelling is expensive and subjective. Swiggy's solution is to synthesize one from the other.

Figure 7.2 — Swiggy's self-supervised pair trick. Two verified-good deliveries become four training rows: the original two stay labelled 0 (accurate), and the geohash-swapped pair get labelled 1 (inaccurate) because the new location and the unchanged text cannot correspond to the same place.

The same idea, seen through colour. Each delivery has a pin (📍) and its text (📝); we colour-code them by the place they describe — blue for HSR, orange for Koramangala. In a real delivery the pin and the text share a colour. Swap the pins across pairs and the row's two colours no longer match — and that mismatch is the label.

Figure 7.2a — The same swap trick in a single glance. The text stays put; only the pin moves across pairs. When the two chips in a row stop sharing a colour, that row becomes a label-1 example — for free.
Why this works

If geohash tdr1wtz reliably sees deliveries addressed as "HSR sector 4", then putting that same text next to a totally different geohash (9q8yyzd, which maps to Koramangala) is by construction a dissonant pair. The label is true by the process that generated it — no annotator opinion required.

The subtle constraint
Swiggy's Part 2 paper notes that this swap must preserve the reverse-geocode relationship — i.e. when you swap geohashes, you should also swap the reverse-geocoded components so the synthetic positive has internally consistent wrong data, not just random wrong data. Otherwise the model learns a shortcut that breaks in production.
Dry run — generating training data from delivery logs
>>> # Start with successfully-delivered orders (implicitly accurate) >>> df_ok = snowflake.read_sql(""" ... SELECT order_id, captured_lat, captured_lng, address_text, pincode ... FROM orders ... WHERE delivery_status = 'DELIVERED' ... AND dp_calls_to_customer = 0 ... AND stop_to_pin_m < 30 ... AND delivery_date >= DATEADD(day, -90, CURRENT_DATE) ... """) >>> len(df_ok) 2_847_213 # 90 days of clean successes >>> # Label these as negatives (location and text agree) >>> df_neg = df_ok.copy() >>> df_neg["label"] = 0 >>> # Now create positives by swapping geohashes across the dataset >>> df_pos = df_ok.copy() >>> df_pos["captured_lat"] = df_ok["captured_lat"].sample(frac=1).values >>> df_pos["captured_lng"] = df_ok["captured_lng"].sample(frac=1).values >>> df_pos["label"] = 1 >>> # Sanity check: make sure we didn't accidentally swap into the same pincode >>> df_pos = df_pos[df_pos["captured_pincode"] != df_pos["pincode"]] >>> df_train = pd.concat([df_neg, df_pos]).sample(frac=1).reset_index(drop=True) >>> df_train["label"].value_counts() 0 2847213 1 2791804 # slightly fewer positives because of pincode filter
Takeaway for Wiom
We can use the exact same trick. Every successful install in our history is an implicit "location agreed with text" label. Swap the lat/lon across pairs of installs in different pincodes, keep the address text, and you have positives. The training set materialises from wiom_data.py — no one needs to label anything. This is the fastest path from "we don't have training data" to "we have a classifier."

7.4 · Shiprocket · Hugging Face · 2025

BIO-tagged Indian address NER — how token classification actually works

The Shiprocket model isn't magic; it's BERT + a linear head that labels each token as part of a specific address entity. Here's exactly what it does to Priya's address.

Named Entity Recognition in Shiprocket's model follows the BIO (Begin, Inside, Outside) tagging convention used since CoNLL-2003. Every word gets a label:

  • B- prefix means the word begins a new entity span
  • I- prefix means the word is inside (continues) the current span
  • O means the word is outside any entity (filler, separators)

The two-head architecture

Under the hood there's a frozen BERT encoder followed by a small linear classification head. The head outputs a softmax over (2 × num_entity_types + 1) classes — a B and I tag for each entity type, plus the singleton O. Shiprocket's model has 11 entity types → 23 output classes.
